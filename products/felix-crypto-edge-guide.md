## 1. What Crypto Trading Really Is

Crypto trading is buying and selling digital assets such as Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, or other coins because you think price may move. Beginners often believe trading is about guessing. A better way to think about trading is:

> Trading is making a plan, defining the risk, waiting for the right area, and accepting that any single trade can lose.

A simple trade idea should answer six questions:

- What is the market doing overall?
- Where did price react before?
- What area gives a reasonable entry?
- Where is the idea wrong?
- How much money is at risk if wrong?
- Is the possible reward worth the possible loss?

You do not need to be right every time. You need a process that protects you when you are wrong.

## 2. The Beginner Mindset

Most new traders lose because they rush. They see a coin pumping, feel fear of missing out, and buy late. Then price pulls back, they panic, and they sell badly.

Focus on three things first:

1. **Survival** - do not blow up your account.
2. **Structure** - follow the same process every time.
3. **Learning** - review trades and improve gradually.

Beginner rule: if you cannot explain why you are entering, where you are wrong, and where you would take profit, you do not have a trade plan.

## 3. Candlesticks Explained Simply

A candle shows what price did during one time period. On a 1-hour chart, each candle shows one hour. On a daily chart, each candle shows one day.

Each candle has:

- **Open** - where price started.
- **Close** - where price ended.
- **High** - the highest price reached.
- **Low** - the lowest price reached.
- **Body** - the distance between open and close.
- **Wick** - price movement above or below the body.

A green candle usually means price closed higher than it opened. A red candle usually means price closed lower than it opened. Long wicks can show rejection: price tried to move into an area, but could not stay there.

Do not trade from one candle alone. A candle matters more when it appears at support, resistance, a trendline, or another important area.

## 4. Support and Resistance Zones

**Support** is an area where price previously stopped falling or bounced. **Resistance** is an area where price previously stopped rising or rejected.

Think zones, not perfect lines. A support zone is not a magical floor. It is an area where buyers showed interest before. A resistance zone is not a guaranteed ceiling. It is an area where sellers showed interest before.

Beginners often make two mistakes:

- Drawing too many levels.
- Treating levels as guaranteed.

Support can break. Resistance can break. The job is not to predict perfectly. The job is to identify areas where a trade idea may make sense, then manage risk.

## 5. Trends and Market Structure

A market can generally be in one of three states:

1. **Uptrend** - price makes higher highs and higher lows.
2. **Downtrend** - price makes lower highs and lower lows.
3. **Range** - price moves sideways between support and resistance.

In an uptrend, beginners often look for pullbacks toward support, a moving average, or a previous breakout area. In a downtrend, buying is riskier because price may keep falling. In a range, buying near support and selling near resistance can be easier to understand than chasing the middle.

Simple rule: do not fight the trend until you understand reversals.

## 6. Timeframes Without Confusion

A timeframe controls how much time each candle represents. A 5-minute chart is noisy. A daily chart is slower and cleaner.

A beginner-friendly structure:

- **Higher timeframe**: use 4-hour or daily charts to understand direction.
- **Planning timeframe**: use 1-hour or 4-hour charts to mark zones.
- **Entry timeframe**: use 15-minute or 1-hour charts only after the plan is clear.

Do not jump between timeframes to find an excuse to enter. Start big, then zoom in only if the big picture makes sense.

## 7. Volume, Moving Averages, and RSI

Indicators are tools. They are not magic.

### Volume

Volume shows how much trading happened. A breakout with higher volume can be more meaningful than a breakout with weak volume. Low volume can mean the move is less convincing.

### Moving Averages

A moving average smooths price. Common examples include the 20-period, 50-period, and 200-period moving averages.

Possible beginner uses:

- Price above major moving averages can suggest bullish structure.
- Price below major moving averages can suggest bearish structure.
- Moving averages can act as dynamic support or resistance.

### RSI

RSI stands for Relative Strength Index. It measures momentum.

- RSI above 70 may mean price is overextended.
- RSI below 30 may mean price is oversold.
- RSI near the middle is often neutral.

Do not buy only because RSI is low. Do not sell only because RSI is high. Use indicators with market structure.

## 8. Entry, Exit, Stop-Loss, and Target

A trade plan needs four parts:

1. **Entry** - where you enter.
2. **Stop-loss** - where you exit if wrong.
3. **Target** - where you may take profit.
4. **Position size** - how much you risk.

A stop-loss is not a failure. It is the planned exit for a trade idea that did not work. Without a stop-loss, one bad trade can become a disaster.

---

## 9. Position Sizing and Risk

Risk management is more important than finding perfect entries.

Example:

- Account size: EUR 1,000
- Risk per trade: 1%
- Maximum planned loss: EUR 10

If your stop-loss is far away, your position size must be smaller. If your stop-loss is close, your position size can be larger, but the trade may be easier to stop out. Beginners should keep risk small enough that one losing trade does not emotionally damage them.

Simple formula:

> Position size = money risked / distance to stop-loss

## 10. Example: A Planned Long Setup

Fictional example:

1. BTC is in an uptrend on the 4-hour chart.
2. Price pulls back to a previous support zone.
3. A candle rejects the zone and closes back above it.
4. Entry is planned after confirmation.
5. Stop-loss goes below the support zone.
6. Target is near the next resistance zone.
7. Risk is 1% of account.
8. Reward is at least 2 times the planned risk.

This does not mean the trade will win. It means the trade has a clear reason and a controlled loss if wrong.

## 11. Example: When to Skip a Trade

Skipping bad trades is a real skill.

Skip when:

- Price has already pumped hard and you feel FOMO.
- You cannot identify support or resistance.
- The stop-loss would be too far away.
- The target is too close compared with the risk.
- You are angry after a loss.
- You are only entering because someone online said to buy.

A missed trade is not a loss. A forced trade can become one.

Felix Crypto Edge should be used as a learning assistant, not a profit machine.

2. Select a higher timeframe and label the trend.

## 13. Trading Checklist

Before any trade, answer:

- What coin am I trading?
- What timeframe am I using?
- Is the market trending or ranging?
- Where is support?
- Where is resistance?
- Why is this entry interesting?
- Where is my stop-loss?
- Where is my target?
- How much money am I risking?
- Am I calm, or am I chasing?

If you cannot answer, skip the trade.

---

## 14. Common Beginner Mistakes

Avoid these:

- Buying after a huge pump because of fear of missing out.
- Trading with no stop-loss.
- Risking too much on one trade.
- Taking random signals from social media.
- Using too many indicators.
- Revenge trading after a loss.
- Moving the stop-loss because you do not want to be wrong.
- Thinking a tool can guarantee profits.

A tool can help with structure, but discipline still matters.

## 15. Mini Glossary

**Altcoin** - any crypto asset that is not Bitcoin.  
**Breakout** - price moving beyond a support or resistance area.  
**Confirmation** - extra evidence before entering, such as a close above a level.  
**FOMO** - fear of missing out; chasing because price is moving fast.  
**Liquidity** - how easily an asset can be bought or sold.  
**Long** - a trade that benefits if price rises.  
**Pullback** - a temporary move against the trend.  
**Range** - sideways market between support and resistance.  
**Resistance** - area where price may struggle to move higher.  
**Risk/reward** - comparison between possible loss and possible profit.  
**Short** - a trade that benefits if price falls; risky for beginners.  
**Stop-loss** - planned exit if the trade idea is wrong.  
**Support** - area where price may stop falling or bounce.  
**Volatility** - how fast and strongly price moves.
